Miller Beach sits at the southernmost point of Lake Michigan — a place where Indigenous peoples held sacred ceremonies, where escaped slaves claimed land, where aviation was invented in a dune, and where Nobel Prize-winning authors wrote their greatest works. Most people drive right past it.

Stop 01 · Trail Start
South Shore Miller Station
🚂
The 1877 Railroad Strike
First dunes spot beyond the Calumet Industrial Region

A Chicago railroad strike in the late 1800s forced a rail station to be built in Miller — making it the very first dunes destination beyond the South Side's industrial belt. This single decision transformed Miller Beach into a gateway to nature for generations of Chicagoans. The station also sits directly on the historic Underground Railroad Freedom Trail from Chicago to Detroit.

Stop 02 · Business District
6th Avenue & The Swedish Settlement
🏪
Swedish Immigrant Entrepreneurs
Founders of Miller's commercial heart

This map of the business district tells the story of an era when Swedish immigrants from Chicago built storefronts, schools, and a church that still stands as the oldest Lutheran building in Lake County. Miller School Shops, Ayers Realty, Miller Pizza, the original Miller Bakery — buildings representing architectural history of the entire Miller era.

🏛 Architecture
Stop 03 · National Park Gateway
Douglas Nature Center
🌿
Senator Paul H. Douglas
Champion who saved the Indiana Dunes from development

Where the Indiana Dunes National Park was founded. The center is named for Illinois Senator Paul H. Douglas, who fought for decades to protect these dunes from industrial development. The land was donated by US Steel — reclaimed by activist Drusilla Carr who stopped US Steel from developing it. Global rare ecosystems exist here that once belonged to the Potawatomi people, who owned all of Miller until the 1830s.

🌿 Native History 🏖 Indiana Dunes
Stop 04 · Lake Street Beach
Drusilla Carr's Land
👑
Drusilla Carr Squatter Queen
Freed enslaved woman who claimed the dunes — and kept them

One of the most remarkable stories in the dunes. Drusilla Carr — friend and assistant to escaped enslaved man Davy Crockett — lived here in Miller Beach's earliest days. She claimed this land as her own, loaned her dunes for Octave Chanute's historic aviation experiments, and when she was ready, simply kicked everyone else off "her land." A freedom story hiding in plain sight.

Stop 05 · Chanute Trail
Where Flight Was Invented
✈️
Octave Chanute & Davy Crockett
Aviation pioneer and the freed man who made it possible

These dunes were the test site for hundreds of glider experiments by aviation pioneer Octave Chanute — experiments that directly influenced the Wright Brothers. What's less known is that Davy Crockett, a formerly enslaved man, lived here and assisted Chanute throughout. The dunes' perfect wind conditions and soft landings made this the ideal laboratory for the birth of flight.

✊ Black History ✈ Aviation
Stop 06 · Interpretive Sign
Willa Brown — Innovator
⭐
Willa Brown
First Black woman with a commercial pilot's license in the US

Willa Brown was not just a pilot — she was an innovator who built an entire training program. As a Tuskegee Trainer during WWII, she helped shape the generation of Black aviators who would prove themselves over Europe and the Pacific. She was also a Gary Public Schools teacher — her impact rippling from the classroom to the skies. This sign marks the place where her story is told.

✊ Black History ⭐ Women's History
Stop 07 · Historic Landmark
Octave Chanute Memorial & Gary Bath House
🛩
Octave Chanute
Aviation pioneer — his Miller Beach experiments preceded the Wright Brothers

The Gary Bath House, built in 1921, honors both Octave Chanute and the Tuskegee Airmen. Chanute conducted hundreds of experimental glider flights from these dunes — documentation of which was shared with the Wright Brothers and other aviation pioneers globally. The building now serves as an event venue with a lookout area, dedicated to his memory and the history of flight born on these sands.

✈ Aviation 🏛 Architecture
Stop 08 · Memorial
Tuskegee Airmen Memorial
✈️
Quentin P. Smith & Willa Brown
Tuskegee Airman, principal — and the woman who trained them

Quentin B. Smith served as both a Tuskegee Airman and as a Gary Public Schools principal — a life of service in the air and on the ground. Willa Brown, the first Black woman to earn a commercial pilot's license, was a Tuskegee trainer and Gary teacher. The memorial honors their service and the long relationship between Miller Beach, aviation, and Black excellence that stretches back to Octave Chanute's experiments on these same dunes.

✊ Black History ⭐ Women's History ✈ Aviation
Stop 09 · Marquette Park
The Fight for the Beach
✊
1949 Integration Protest
Miller Beach was redlined from the 1920s to the 1960s

Miller was a "sundown town" — Black people were barred from the beach and from living here. In 1949, a pivotal protest forced integration of the park and beach access. The struggle continued into the Civil Rights era, when Miller became a remarkable story of integration in a region defined by racial division. This park was the frontline of that fight.

✊ Civil Rights
Stop 10 · Sacred Ground
Indigenous Peoples Sacred Site
🌊
The Potawatomi People
Owners of all Miller Beach until the Indian Removal Act, 1830s

The southernmost point of Lake Michigan was a sacred gathering place for Indigenous nations — people came here from across the region to trade and join in ceremonies. The historic gates flanking this point were built by the Civilian Conservation Corps after the Great Depression. The Grand Calumet River once flowed freely to Lake Michigan here. The Potawatomi owned all of Miller until they were forcibly removed.

🌿 Native History
Stop 11 · Cemetery
Bethel Lutheran Cemetery
🌸
Drusilla Carr & the Potawatomi
Early Pottawatomie People and Swedish settlers rest here together

The oldest Lutheran church in Lake County — built in 1894 for a growing Swedish immigrant community — holds this cemetery where Drusilla Carr is buried alongside early Potawatomi people and victims of the Aetna Powder Plant explosion. A final resting place where the layers of Miller Beach's history lie side by side: the Indigenous, the immigrant, the freed, the fallen.

🌿 Native History ⭐ Women's History
Stop 12 · Memorial
Father Marquette Memorial
⛵
Father Jacques Marquette
French explorer, 1675 — guided by Miami people around the Great Lakes

French explorer Father Marquette, sent by Louis XIV, camped at the mouth of the Grand Calumet River in what is now Marquette Park in 1675 — before he left for Michigan where he passed away. He traveled with 4 Miami guides throughout the Great Lakes region. The river he camped beside once connected directly to Lake Michigan here, at the continent's crossroads.

🌿 Native History ⛵ Exploration
Stop 13 · Historic Venue
Marquette Pavilion
🏛
US Steel's Grand Gift
Donated to Gary — and the community that fought to keep it public

The grand Marquette Pavilion was donated by US Steel at the park's inception. The park and buildings were gifted to the City of Gary in exchange for the Grand Calumet River being piped underground to make land for the steel plant. Recently restored, its grand ballroom, outdoor patio, and dance pavilion are now available for events — a place of contradictions, beauty, and history.

🏛 Architecture
Stop 14 · Literary Landmark
Nelson Algren's Cottage
📖
Nelson Algren & Simone de Beauvoir
He wrote "Man with a Golden Arm" here. She wrote "The Second Sex."

Award-winning Chicago author Nelson Algren bought this lagoon cottage with his earnings from "Man with the Golden Arm" and lived here for 17 years. His French lover — philosopher and feminist Simone de Beauvoir — wrote parts of her landmark work "The Second Sex" during her summer visits. A Miller Beach cottage that quietly hosted two of the 20th century's most important literary minds.

⭐ Women's History 📚 Literature
Stop 15 · Greenway
Grand Boulevard & the Sand Railroad
🏭
The Ball Glass Railroad
How Indiana's dunes were mined to build America's industry

This former railroad line served a remarkable and largely unknown purpose — it transported mined sand from the Lake Michigan coastal dunes to important Indiana companies like Ball Glass, whose jars became iconic American objects. Entire dunes were torn down and moved by rail. The ramp from Grand Boulevard to the Marquette Greenway follows this former industrial corridor, now reclaimed as a trail.

🏭 Industrial History
Stop 16 · Final Stop
Town of Miller Town Hall
🏛
Miller's Independence
The only town hall from before Miller was annexed by Gary

The first and only town hall built when Miller was an independent municipality — before it was annexed by Gary as a neighborhood. Now on the National Register of Historic Places, this building is a physical record of Miller's original identity as its own town, with its own governance, its own vision of what a lakeshore community could be.

🏛 Architecture
